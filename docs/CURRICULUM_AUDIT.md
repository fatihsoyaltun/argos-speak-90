# Argos Speak 90 Curriculum Audit

## Executive verdict

The current 90-day program is a strong practical scaffold and is likely to help a beginner or low-level learner move toward more confident spoken English in familiar daily and work situations. It is not just a generic English list: the daily flow consistently connects listening, vocabulary chunks, speaking prompts, and review around everyday output.

The program can reasonably move a committed user from A1 foundations toward strong A2 and functional early B1 behaviors in familiar daily/work contexts: describing routines, asking for help, giving simple reasons, explaining small problems, summarizing short situations, and speaking for 45-75 seconds on familiar topics.

However, it should not be framed as a guaranteed full B1 outcome. It is still too controlled, too transcript-dependent, and too exact-answer based in review. Later days ask for early B1-oriented output, but the support system does not always force enough spontaneous production, interaction repair, longer connected speech, or flexible answer checking.

### What is strong

- Complete Day 1-90 coverage exists for listening, words, speaking, review, and the content plan.
- The progression is coherent: daily life -> routine/work language -> reasons and comparisons -> short summaries and opinions.
- Speaking prompts are present every day and become more demanding over time.
- Vocabulary is mostly useful chunks, not isolated random words.
- The app supports repeated output: first try, second try, review, and journal.
- Team pilot use is plausible because daily output is short and trackable.

### What is weak

- Review tasks are mostly exact recall from the transcript, not flexible active production.
- Listening output prompts stay at "2 sentences" even in later days, so listening does not scale with speaking level.
- Days 15-90 rely heavily on generated prompt templates, which gives consistency but reduces pedagogical variety.
- Vocabulary expansion uses rotating booster chunks. This creates useful repetition, but some booster items feel only loosely connected to the day theme.
- There is limited interaction practice: turn-taking, follow-up questions, repair, clarification loops, and role-play are underdeveloped.
- Later B1 goals need more short connected speech: story, opinion, reason, example, contrast, and next step in one answer.

### Must fix before serious team use

1. Make review less transcript-recitation and more production-oriented.
2. Strengthen later-day speaking tasks so users cannot finish with only safe, short answers.
3. Audit booster vocabulary for theme fit and harmful repetition.
4. Add clearer milestone assessments at Days 14, 42, 70, and 90.
5. Prepare a future domain bridge for work/device English without inserting ForenScope content yet.

- self-introduction
- preferences
- simple work/study responsibility
- basic questions
- plans
- past simple experiences
- polite help requests
- simple opinions
- place descriptions
- short messages

Speaking output expectation:

- Mostly 2-4 short sentences.
- Appropriate for A1/A1+.
- Day 14 gives a useful confidence checkpoint.

- Strong for beginner speaking.

Difficulty progression:

Verdict:

- This range is the strongest match to the target learner. It feels practical, calm, and achievable.

- schedule conflicts
- recommendations
- detailed place descriptions
- work decisions
- late replies
- short stories
- problem/options

- confidence reflection
- final speaking check

Speaking output expectation:

- 4-6 sentences, 60-75 seconds, with context, point, reason/example, and next step.

Vocabulary usefulness:

- Many useful chunks: "the main risk", "a clear priority", "from experience", "a stronger answer", "to summarize", "real conversation".
- Some final-stage chunks are strong, but the curriculum needs more real conversation tasks to make them active.

Review quality:

- Too controlled for late-stage B1 preparation.
- Review should include paraphrase, choose a response, fix a message, give a reason, and answer with personal context.

Difficulty progression:

- Day 90 is a good confidence checkpoint, but it should include a more explicit real-world speaking task.

Verdict:

- This range points toward early B1, but it needs more spontaneous interaction and less exact recall to be credible as "functional early B1 in familiar daily/work contexts".

## Speaking-first check

### Days 1-14

Users are asked to produce real output early. The output is short, realistic, and low-pressure. This is appropriate.

Risk:

- Some users may copy target lines instead of producing their own sentences.

Fix:

- Add review/speaking prompts that require at least one personal detail.

### Days 15-42

The generated speaking prompt asks for 3-4 then 4-5 sentences. This is a good speaking-first structure.

Risk:

- The template is repeated too often: state situation, add detail, finish with reason/next step. This builds a useful habit, but it can become predictable.

Fix:

- Add rotating speaking modes: message, mini role-play, explanation, comparison, apology, request, recommendation.

### Days 43-70

The speaking expectations are appropriate for early B1: 4-5 sentences with context and reason/example/next step.

Risk:

- Users can still write a safe paragraph without practicing interaction, clarification, or response under constraint.

Fix:

- Add prompts like "answer a coworker", "reply to a customer", "ask one follow-up", "correct a misunderstanding".

### Days 71-90

The output target of 4-6 sentences and 60-75 seconds is strong.

Risk:

- The curriculum says "real conversation" but does not yet simulate enough real conversation.

Fix:

- Add late-stage tasks requiring question-answer-turn structure, short presentation opening/closing, and summary plus next action.

## Vocabulary audit

### Strengths

- Every day has 8 vocabulary items.
- Many items are chunks, which is correct for speaking-first learning.
- Vocabulary mostly supports daily speaking functions.
- Early items are accessible.
- Later items include useful early B1 language for reasons, summaries, decisions, and work communication.

### Risks

- Days 15-90 seed only 4 day-specific words, then use 4 booster chunks. Boosters are useful, but not always tightly theme-matched.
- The rotating booster system creates visible repetition patterns.
- Some generic chunks can become filler if not attached to the daily speaking task.

Examples of repeated booster items:

- Days 15/20/26/37: "next step"
- Days 15/26/32/37: "short break"
- Days 16/21/27/38: "in the right order"
- Days 17/22/28/39: "follow the plan"
- Days 43/48/54/65: "agree on"
- Days 43/54/60/65: "make a decision"
- Days 43/49/54/60: "what matters most"

These are not automatically bad. Some repetition is useful spaced repetition. The issue is that the repetition appears algorithmic rather than intentionally tied to the day.

### Fix direction

- Keep 8 items per day.
- Make at least 6 of 8 items tightly theme-specific.
- Reserve 2 items for intentional spaced repetition.
- Mark repeated chunks as "review chunk" in future content QA, or replace them with theme-specific phrases.

## Review audit

### Strengths

- Every day has review.
- Review items are checkable.
- Items usually connect to the day's transcript.
- Fill-blank, recall, and shortAnswer types are simple and mobile-friendly.

### Weaknesses

- Review mostly tests exact transcript recall.
- Many expected answers require a precise sentence rather than allowing natural alternatives.
- Later review does not scale enough for B1-like flexible output.
- Review rarely asks the learner to apply vocabulary to a new situation.

### Fix direction

For early days:

- Keep exact recall but add one personal sentence task.

For mid days:

- Add transformation tasks: "Make this more polite", "Add one reason", "Change this to yesterday", "Ask a follow-up question".

For later days:

- Add production tasks: "Write a 3-sentence response", "Choose a soft opinion line", "Summarize the decision and next step".

## Repetition audit

### Useful spaced repetition

The program intentionally repeats high-value speaking moves:

- asking for help
- making plans
- giving reasons
- explaining problems
- checking understanding
- summarizing progress
- speaking about confidence

This repetition is healthy because these functions are central to spoken English.

### Harmful or questionable repetition

- Some key lines repeat exactly or almost exactly, for example "I prefer short meetings" across Days 23 and 44.
- "My schedule changed this week" appears in separate later contexts.
- The prompt template repeats heavily from Days 15 onward.
- Booster chunks repeat in a mechanical rotation and should be reviewed for theme fit.

### Fix direction

- Keep functional repetition.
- Reduce exact repeated lines unless the second use intentionally upgrades the structure.
- Replace repeated generic boosters with domain-specific or scenario-specific chunks.

## Work/technical English readiness

The current program is a good base for later work/device-specific English but is not ready for ForenScope or device communication yet.

Current readiness:

- Strong foundation in simple work communication: updates, delays, meetings, files, deadlines, requests, instructions, clarification, problems, and options.
- Good general language for explaining issues calmly.
- Some technical-adjacent language appears: file, link, document, load, format, final version, attachment, check details.

Missing before ForenScope/device content:

- device state language: on/off, connected, disconnected, charging, battery, signal, calibration, scan, result
- procedure language: attach, remove, wait, confirm, restart, test, record
- troubleshooting language: error message, failed, not responding, try again, replace, report
- customer/support language: explain the issue simply, ask for permission, confirm next step
- safety/accuracy language: check twice, avoid mistakes, record clearly, share results

Recommended insertion points later, without adding device content now:

- Days 47-48: technical problem and clarification
- Days 56-57: mistake and feedback
- Days 63-64: checking understanding and process explanation
- Days 68-70: needs and polite instructions
- Days 71, 80, 85, 88: priorities, work decision, problem/options, discussion summary

## Red flags: top 10 content risks

1. Review remains too exact-answer based for an early B1-oriented speaking goal.
2. Listening tasks do not scale beyond short, controlled excerpts.
3. Later speaking prompts are structurally repetitive.
4. Vocabulary boosters can feel algorithmic instead of lesson-specific.
5. Interaction practice is underdeveloped.
6. There is limited repair language: "I mean", "Let me say that again", "What I meant was..."
7. There is no explicit fluency-building loop beyond first try and second try.
8. No milestone rubric clearly defines what "good enough" means at Days 14, 42, 70, and 90.
9. The curriculum approaches early B1 in familiar contexts but may overclaim if presented as guaranteed full B1 readiness.
10. Work/technical English foundation exists, but device-specific readiness is not yet mapped into the 90-day path.

## Prioritized fix plan

### Must fix before team pilot

1. Review QA pass: make at least one review item per day production-based, not just recall.
2. Vocabulary QA pass: identify repeated booster chunks and decide which are intentional spaced repetition.
3. Milestone checks: add clear Day 14, Day 42, Day 70, and Day 90 success criteria.
4. Later speaking prompts: add more task variety to Days 43-90.
5. Team pilot instructions: tell users not to only copy target lines; they must add personal detail.

### Should fix after 1 week of pilot data

1. Review real user journal answers to see where prompts are too easy.
2. Shorten or simplify days that feel too long on phone.
3. Replace repeated chunks that users ignore or find boring.
4. Add more follow-up question practice.
5. Add more "repair and clarify" language in the middle and late phases.

### Optional later improvements

1. Add optional role-play cards.
2. Add spoken self-rating rubrics.
3. Add domain tracks after the base curriculum: ForenScope/device support, customer explanation, field troubleshooting.
4. Add listening variants with more natural hesitation and accents.
5. Add flexible answer checking instead of exact string matching.

## Final audit verdict

Argos Speak 90 has the right skeleton for a serious speaking-first product. It is coherent, complete, practical, and much stronger than a generic word-list app. It can plausibly help users become more confident and more functional in familiar spoken English.

The main issue is not coverage. The issue is depth of production. To become credible as a 90-day path toward functional early B1 in familiar daily/work contexts, it needs stronger review tasks, less template repetition, more interaction practice, and clearer milestone assessment. The curriculum should not be described as a guaranteed full B1 outcome.
